Seasons

Spring

春 / haru

Four thousand trees light up at once.

April — May
The snow pulls back in April, and what it leaves behind is extraordinary. Four thousand cherry trees along a castle moat, three thousand paper lanterns, and the reflection of both in still water. Higher up, the rice terraces fill and become mirrors. And at Japan's oldest vineyard, the man who crossbred ten thousand grape varieties left behind a stone cellar that still keeps its wine cold with snow.
Spring

What the thaw reveals.

— 01

Takada Castle cherry blossoms

高田城の桜 / Takada-jō no sakura

Four thousand Somei Yoshino trees and three thousand bonbori lanterns line the moats of Takada Castle, built in 1614 for Tokugawa Ieyasu's sixth son. CNN named it one of the world's five best flower-viewing festivals. The 300-metre Sakura Road, reflected in the moat water, is the centrepiece. The trees were first planted in 1909 by a veterans' association — the festival began in 1926 and celebrated its hundredth anniversary in 2025.

— 02

Hoshitoge Rice Terraces

星峠の棚田 / Hoshitōge no tanada

Two hundred terraced paddies near Tokamachi, fifty minutes south. In late April, the paddies fill with snowmelt and become mizu-kagami — water mirrors. At sunrise, mist rises from the valley floor, creating a sea of clouds beneath rows of mirrored sky. Photographers arrive before dawn. The rest of the year, they're just rice fields.

— 03

Iwanohara Vineyard

岩の原葡萄園 / Iwanohara budōen

Japan's oldest winery, founded in 1890 by Zenbei Kawakami — the 'father of Japanese wine.' He crossbred 10,311 grape varieties to develop twenty-two suited to snowy Niigata, including Muscat Bailey A, still one of Japan's signature grapes. The No. 1 Ishikura stone cellar is a registered Tangible Cultural Property. The No. 2 cellar uses yukimuro — a snow-based natural refrigerator.

— 04

Naena Falls in snowmelt

苗名滝 / Naena-daki

The 55-metre waterfall on the Sekigawa River is one of Japan's hundred most famous. In spring, snowmelt from the mountains amplifies its power dramatically. The locals call it 'Earthquake Falls' — jishin-daki — for the sound it makes. A flat fifteen-minute walk from the car park.

— 05

Mizubasho at Imori Pond

いもり池の水芭蕉 / Imori-ike no mizubasho

A hundred thousand wild Asian skunk cabbage bloom around this small pond at the foot of Mt. Myoko from mid-April to early May. A boardwalk circles the 500-metre circumference. On a windless morning, the pond holds a perfect mirror of the volcano above. It is one of those places that looks exactly the way Japan is supposed to look.

"Four thousand lanterns, four thousand cherry trees, and a castle moat that holds the reflection of both — Takada in April is Japan at its most unguarded."
