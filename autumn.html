Seasons

Autumn

秋 / aki

The clearest skies of the year.

September — November
The rice comes in. The sake that spent the summer settling in dark tanks is released — rounder, deeper, more patient than winter's fresh-pressed shiboritate. The mountain starts at the top with red and works its way down over six weeks, giving you time. And off the coast, the cold yellowtail begin their migration south, arriving fat and silver in the nets at Naoetsu.
Autumn

What the harvest brings.

— 01

Myoko foliage

妙高の紅葉 / Myōkō no kōyō

The colour begins at the summit of Sasagamine in early October and descends through six weeks — beech and birch turning first, then maple and oak, then the low forests around Imori Pond by late October. At Imori, the pond becomes a mirror of the burning mountain. At Naena Falls, the 55-metre cascade drops through a frame of red and gold basalt walls into late November. You don't need to time it perfectly. You just need to pick an altitude.

— 02

Rice harvest

新米 / shinmai

Niigata is Japan's top rice prefecture, and the reason is here: extreme day-night temperature swings, snowmelt water from the mountains, and alluvial soil built over centuries. Koshihikari — the cultivar that most of Japan considers the best rice grown — was developed in Niigata in 1956, bred specifically for these conditions. Shinmai, the new rice, arrives from late September: higher moisture, pronounced sweetness, a plump stickiness that needs nothing but a bowl.

— 03

Hiyaoroshi sake

ひやおろし / hiyaoroshi

Brewed in winter, pasteurised once in spring, then left to settle through the long summer in dark tanks. When it emerges in autumn, it is a different sake — rounder, deeper, more composed. The term is hiyaoroshi: sake shipped cold. Aki-agari describes sake that specifically improved through summer ageing. Joetsu has twelve breweries within city limits, and in October, thirty of them gather under the gangi arcades for the Echigo Kenshin SAKE Festival — forty thousand visitors, three-thousand-yen day pass.

— 04

Cold yellowtail

寒ブリ / kanburi

The kanburi migration begins off the Joetsu coast in late autumn. The fish arrive fat from their journey south, at peak oil content, silver-bellied and heavy. At Naoetsu's waterfront izakaya, they're served as sashimi, grilled with salt, simmered in buri-daikon — the radish absorbing the fish fat until both become something richer than either started. The season is short. The memory isn't.

— 05

Imori Pond in autumn

秋のいもり池 / Aki no Imori-ike

Five hundred metres around, at the foot of Mt. Myoko. In mid-to-late October, the maples and beeches that ring the pond turn, and on a still morning the water holds a perfect double — the volcano above and the fire below, reflected in a surface that doesn't move. A boardwalk circles it. There is nowhere to sit that isn't beautiful.

"In October, the mountain is on fire and the rice is in — and in Joetsu, the only appropriate response to both is a glass of hiyaoroshi at room temperature."
— From the editors
