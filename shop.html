Things worth carrying home.

What's here
Every product on this page has a story that outlasts the product itself. A chili paste that spends three years fermenting after being bleached on January snow. Sake pressed from rice grown in the same valley where the water that brews it fell as snow on a sacred volcano. Lotus tea blended from flowers that bloom on a castle moat — a moat that exists because a domain went bankrupt in 1871 and planted lotus to survive. These are not souvenirs. They are the place, compressed into something you can carry.

What to bring home.

— 01

Kanzuri

かんずり / kanzuri

Snow-fermented chili paste, made by a single family company in Myoko City. Four ingredients: togarashi chili, yuzu, koji, salt. The peppers are harvested in summer, salt-marinated through autumn, then spread on fresh snow in January for three to four days — the osmosis draws out bitterness. Mixed with koji and yuzu, it ferments for a minimum of three years, some barrels up to six, each turned by hand once a year. The factory is a fifteen-minute walk from Arai Station. Tastings are free.

— 02

Joetsu sake

上越の地酒 / Jōetsu no jizake

Twelve breweries within city limits. Joetsu's water comes from Myoko snowmelt, filtered through granite until it's soft and nearly iron-free — ideal for the clean, dry tanrei karakuchi style that defines Niigata sake. The Echigo Toji guild is one of Japan's three great brewer's guilds. Katafune, from Takeda Shuzoten in Naoetsu, won the IWC Trophy in 2013 and 2015. Ski-Masamune is open year-round for tours and tastings. Setchubai hasn't allowed visitors in forty years — but their sake is worth the mystery.

— 03

Setsuren-cha lotus tea

雪蓮茶 / setsuren-cha

Blended from lotus harvested on the outer moat of Takada Castle — nineteen hectares of flowers on water that was ice four months earlier. The moat's lotus began as a survival crop after the domain collapsed. Now it's the city's most recognisable summer image and the source of a tea that tastes like something between green tea and dried flowers, with a finish that lingers.

— 04

Okina-ame & Sasa dango

翁飴・笹団子 / okina-ame, sasa dango

Okina-ame is a traditional Joetsu sweet — mizuame and kanten, translucent and chewy, unchanged for generations. Sasa dango is five hundred years old: yomogi mochi wrapped around red bean paste, bundled in bamboo leaves. Possibly Uesugi Kenshin's battlefield rations — portable, preserved, and genuinely delicious. Kotake Seika in Joetsu, founded 1924, makes them and a sasa-dango bread that shouldn't work but does.

— 05

Myoko Kogen Beer

妙高高原ビール / Myōkō Kōgen Bīru

A craft brewery on the Myoko highland, brewing since 1997. The brewer trained in Prague. The water is Myoko snowmelt. It is the kind of local beer that exists because someone cared enough to make it good, not because someone saw a market opportunity.

— 06

Gangi arcade shopping

雁木の商店街 / Gangi no shōtengai

Sixteen kilometres of covered wooden arcades — the longest in Japan — thread through old Takada. The Honmachi stretch runs 750 metres with roughly two hundred specialty shops: dye shops, coopers, sweet makers, sake cellars. In October, the arcades host the Echigo Kenshin SAKE Festival: thirty-plus booths, forty thousand visitors, and a three-thousand-yen day pass that proves this town takes its drinking seriously.

Online shop launching summer 2026. In the meantime, most of these products can be found at Sakura Plaza (Joetsu-Myoko Station) and along the gangi arcades of Takada.
