Stay

泊 / tomaru

Hot springs, old wood, and the sound of snow falling.

What's here
The accommodation in this region maps the landscape itself: onsen ryokan on volcanic slopes where the mineral water changes colour from one valley to the next. Century-old wooden houses in castle towns where the beams are thicker than your arm. Mountain huts where a hundred hikers share a sunset. And one resort built by a Sony heir, abandoned, seized for taxes, bought by a Korean conglomerate, and reopened as Japan's best ski resort. Where you sleep here is part of the story.

Where the story continues.

— 01

Myoko's seven hot springs

妙高七湯 / Myōkō nana-yu

Seven onsen, seven different mineral waters, each with its own character. Akakura's calcium-rich dual spring is called 'beautiful skin water.' Seki's rust-red iron bath — the oldest in Myoko, opened 1729 — has a heating effect that lingers hours after you leave. Tsubame's milky white sulphur baths sit at the trailhead of Mt. Myoko, open to the sky. Ikenotaira has rare black mineral water. No other area this compact offers this range of waters.

— 02

Kominka stays

古民家 / kominka

The old castle towns and mountain villages of this region still have their wooden houses — thick-beamed, irori-hearted, tatami-floored structures that are a hundred or more years old. Some have been turned into guesthouses, furnished simply, with futon on tatami and sliding screens that open to gardens or mountain views. In central Takada, these houses sit within walking distance of the castle, the gangi arcades, and the evening pub streets. In Myoko, they're closer to the trailheads and the snow.

— 03

Akakura Kanko Hotel

赤倉観光ホテル / Akakura Kankō Hoteru

Founded in 1937 by the Okura zaibatsu at one thousand metres on Myoko's slopes. White Alpine-style architecture with views to Nojiri Lake and, on clear days, Sado Island. Ski-in, ski-out in winter. A SPA & SUITE wing arrived in 2009, a Premium wing in 2016. It remains Myoko's most distinguished address — the kind of place where the building tells you it has been there longer than you, and will still be there when you leave.

— 04

Tsubame Onsen wild baths

燕温泉の野天風呂 / Tsubame Onsen no noten-buro

Two free outdoor baths at the Mt. Myoko trailhead. Ougon-no-yu, the Golden Bath, is gender-separated. Kawara-no-yu sits on a cliff edge next to the river — mixed bathing, no walls, milky white water, and nothing between you and the mountain. Open roughly June through December, depending on the snow. There is no changing room at Kawara-no-yu. There is no pretence. It is the mountain's bath, and you are a guest.

— 05

Lotte Arai Resort

ロッテアライリゾート / Lotte Arai Rizōto

Originally opened in 1993 by Hideo Morita, son of Sony co-founder Akio Morita. It closed in 2006, was seized by the city for unpaid taxes, and bought by Lotte for 1.8 billion yen in 2016. It reopened in December 2017 with a thousand hectares of skiable terrain — and won Japan's Best Ski Resort at the 2020 World Ski Awards. An hour forty-six from Tokyo by Shinkansen. Rates start around seventy dollars a night in the off-season.

— 06

Mountain lodges

山小屋 / yamagoya

For hikers linking Mt. Myoko and Mt. Hiuchi, two mountain huts offer overnight stays. Koya-ike Hutte sits between the peaks at the edge of a marshland pond — a hundred beds, six thousand yen a night, tent camping for nine hundred. Kurosawa-ike Hutte is a distinctive blue dome in the alpine zone, reservation-based, four hours from the Myoko summit. Both are simple. Both are the reason people come back.

"Seven hot springs, seven different waters. A rust-red iron bath that opened in 1729. A milky white cliff-edge soak with nothing between you and the mountain. The accommodation here isn't where you sleep — it's where the story continues."
