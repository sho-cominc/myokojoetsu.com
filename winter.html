Seasons

Winter

冬 / fuyu

The quietest season.

December — March
Thirteen metres of snow fall here between December and March. The mountains disappear. The rooftops double in height. And beneath it all — in brewery cellars, in outdoor baths, in farmhouse kitchens — a different kind of life continues, quietly.
Winter

What the snow brings.

— 01

Powder skiing

パウダー / paudā

Seven resorts line the base of Mt. Myoko, from Seki Onsen's ungroomed two-lift cult classic to Lotte Arai's thousand-hectare luxury. Suginohara holds Japan's longest single run: 8.5 kilometres from summit to base.

— 02

Onsen in snow

温泉 / onsen

Seven distinct hot springs, each with different mineral water — from Akakura's calcium-rich 'beautiful skin water' to Seki's rust-red iron bath, the oldest in Myoko at nearly three centuries. Tsubame's milky white outdoor baths sit at the trailhead of Mt. Myoko, open to the sky and the falling snow.

— 03

New sake

新酒 / shinshu

Joetsu City has twelve sake breweries. Winter is when they press shiboritate — unpasteurized new sake, brash and lively, often available only at the brewery door. The snow keeps the air cold and clean. The water comes from Myoko's springs. Drink it where it was made.

— 04

Kanzuri

かんずり / kanzuri

In January, chili peppers are laid on beds of fresh snow and left for three days. The cold draws out bitterness. Then they're mixed with yuzu and koji and sealed in barrels for three years. Four ingredients, no shortcuts — Myoko's signature condiment.

— 05

The silence

静寂 / seijaku

What thirteen metres of snow actually sounds like: nothing. The snow absorbs everything. The towns go quiet. The forests go quieter. You hear the house settle under the weight. You hear your own breathing. This is what people mean when they say 'snow country.'

"In Myoko, you don't go to the snow — the snow comes to you, silently, relentlessly, thirteen metres of it, and the only sound is the creak of timber under its weight."
— From the editors
