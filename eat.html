Eat

食 / taberu

Food that rarely leaves the prefecture.

What's here
The food here comes from specific conditions — heavy snow, volcanic water, Japan Sea currents, and centuries of making do with what the mountains and the coast provide. The noodles get their texture from seaweed that weavers used in textiles. The ramen uses sake lees stored in snow rooms. The fish arrives fat from its winter migration. Nothing here was invented for visitors. Everything here is the result of people eating well in a place that gets thirteen metres of snow.
Eat

What the place provides.

— 01

Hegi soba

へぎそば / hegi soba

Niigata's signature noodle. Made with funori seaweed instead of wheat flour as a binder, giving a silky, elastic texture with a firm bite. The connection is unexpected: funori was originally used in weaving Ojiya Chijimi textiles — a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. The weavers discovered it made extraordinary noodles. Food culture born from textile culture. Served on rectangular wooden hegi trays, the noodles arranged in elegant bite-sized bundles.

— 02

Yukimuro Sakekasu Ramen

雪むろ酒かすラーメン / yukimuro sakekasu rāmen

Joetsu's own ramen — developed in 2017 and trademarked by the Japanese Patent Office in 2021. The rules are strict: Joetsu-produced sake lees, ingredients stored in snow rooms, topped with fermented foods and local vegetables or seafood. Nineteen establishments across the city serve it, each with their own interpretation. It bridges sake culture and ramen culture in a way that only exists here.

— 03

Japan Sea seafood

日本海の幸 / Nihonkai no sachi

Nodoguro — the 'wagyu of the sea' — is a rich, fatty deep-sea fish that peaks in autumn and winter. Cold yellowtail arrives in December at maximum fat content. At Gunchan near Naoetsu, or Tomi Zushi's Naoetsu branch, the fish comes from the port the same morning. An hour north at Marine Dream Nou, Japan's largest direct-from-boat red snow crab market lets you pick your crab from the tank and eat it on the spot.

— 04

Sake culture

酒文化 / sake bunka

Twelve breweries within Joetsu city limits, twenty in the broader region. The water is Myoko snowmelt filtered through granite — soft, iron-free, ideal for Niigata's defining tanrei karakuchi style: clean, light, dry. The Echigo Toji guild is one of Japan's three great brewer's guilds. In October, the Echigo Kenshin SAKE Festival fills the gangi arcades with thirty booths, forty thousand visitors, and a three-thousand-yen day pass.

— 05

Mountain vegetables

山菜 / sansai

In spring and early summer, the mountains produce warabi, zenmai, wild bamboo shoots — sansai, the mountain vegetables that appear for a few weeks and then are gone. Takenoko-jiru is bamboo shoot and miso soup, a Myoko staple. Sasazushi is vinegared rice on bamboo leaves topped with mountain vegetables and fish — possibly what Uesugi Kenshin's soldiers ate in the field. Noppe-jiru, a root vegetable stew with sesame oil, is the New Year dish no household skips.

— 06

Koshihikari rice

コシヒカリ / Koshihikari

Niigata is Japan's top rice prefecture. Koshihikari — the cultivar most of Japan considers the finest — was developed here in 1956, bred for the extreme day-night temperature swings, snowmelt water, and alluvial soil. Uonuma Koshihikari holds the top 'A' taste ranking for twenty-eight consecutive years. In late September, shinmai arrives — the new rice, higher in moisture, sweeter, stickier, and for a few weeks, the best rice in Japan.

"The noodles get their texture from seaweed that weavers used in textiles. The ramen is trademarked. The rice cultivar was bred specifically for this snow and this soil. Nothing here was designed to travel — which is exactly why it's worth travelling for."
— From the editors
